Hydration

Water, Salt, and Steady Days

By the Steady Living team Mar 26, 2026 8 min read

Plenty of people hit the number on the bottle and still hit a gray 3 p.m. Water was always part of the answer — never the whole answer.

Somewhere along the way, hydration became a scoreboard. A bottle with time markers printed down the side. An app that congratulates you. A number — eight glasses, a gallon, whatever the poster in the gym says — that someone repeated until it turned into a fact. And yet plenty of people hit the number and still spend 3 p.m. staring at a spreadsheet with a low, gray headache, wondering what else they're supposed to be doing. Water is part of the answer. It was never the whole answer.

Why "just drink more" keeps missing

Water doesn't work alone. It moves through your body alongside minerals that carry a charge — sodium, potassium, magnesium, chloride — and those minerals are what let water actually stay where it's useful instead of passing through on its way out. Your kidneys are very good at holding the whole system in balance, and they mostly do it without your input. That's the part the bottle-with-markers crowd skips.

Here's the practical consequence. If you're a little short on those minerals and you respond by drinking a large volume of plain water, fast, you can end up diluting what you had. You feel briefly better, and forty minutes later you're back where you started, only now you've also been to the bathroom four times. Same reason chugging a liter before a long drive doesn't hold you — the water arrives faster than your body can find a use for it.

Thirst is a decent alarm and a terrible dashboard. It tells you when to drink. It tells you almost nothing about whether the last three days added up.

What salt is actually doing in an ordinary day

Sodium has a bad reputation it partly earned and partly inherited. In the context of a normal day, though, it's doing a mundane job: it's the mineral your body uses to decide how much fluid to hold onto. You lose it when you sweat — that's the sting in your eyes after a hot walk and the white crust on a dark shirt. You lose more of it than you think on a summer day where you never once "worked out."

Most people eating regular food get plenty without trying, because it's already in bread, cheese, sauces, and everything that comes in a package. But some patterns quietly cut the supply: cooking everything from scratch, eating very few processed foods, going hard on a low-carb approach, long sessions in the heat, or drinking a lot of water on a day you barely ate. None of those are bad choices. They just change the math, and the math doesn't announce itself.

This is also where the wellness aisle gets loud. Powders, packets, bright colors, big promises. Some are fine and some are sugar with a marketing budget. The useful question isn't which brand — it's whether your day actually took anything out of you worth putting back.

What being a little short tends to feel like

Nobody gets a notification. The signals are boring and easy to blame on something else, which is exactly why they run for weeks. Some of the more common ones people report:

  • A dull, band-around-the-head headache that shows up mid-afternoon, not morning.
  • Standing up fast and getting the gray-static moment before your vision returns.
  • Fatigue that feels flat rather than sleepy — you're not tired, you're unplugged.
  • Cramping in a calf or a foot at night, or during the last ten minutes of a walk.
  • Craving something salty with a specificity that surprises you. Not food. Chips.
  • Peeing constantly and clearly, all day, while still feeling parched.

That last one is the tell that breaks the "drink more" logic. Clear urine every ninety minutes plus a dry mouth is not a person who needs more water. That's water going straight through.

Quick check: Next time the 3 p.m. fog rolls in, don't reach for the bottle first. Have a glass of water with something salty and real — a handful of olives, some cheese, leftover soup, a pinch of salt in the water if that's all you've got. If you feel noticeably better within twenty minutes, water alone was never the missing piece.

Different days ask for different things

The reason a single daily number fails is that no two of your days cost the same. A cool office Tuesday and a Saturday hauling boxes in July are not the same body. Roughly how it shakes out:

The day you're havingWhat it takes out of youWhat tends to help
Desk day, air conditioning, low sweatVery little; normal meals cover itDrink to thirst; nothing special required
Hot day outdoors, lots of walkingFluid and noticeable sodiumWater alongside real food; salt with meals, not just after
Long or sweaty training sessionSodium losses that plain water won't replaceSomething with sodium during or right after; a salty meal counts
Very clean, low-processed, low-carb eatingLess incidental sodium than you're used toSalt your own cooking deliberately; don't assume "less is better"
Coffee-heavy morning, breakfast skippedFluid in, minerals never showed upPair the caffeine with food; see the timing piece below

None of that is a prescription. It's a way of asking the right question — what did today actually cost? — instead of chasing a number that was invented for a poster.

A rhythm that doesn't need an app

The people who stay steady on this rarely think about it. They just have habits with the minerals built in. A glass of water when you wake up, with breakfast rather than instead of it. Water with meals, because that's when the sodium and potassium show up anyway and the two work better together. A real drink after anything that made you sweat, not a token sip. And salt used like an ingredient, not like a moral failure — on the eggs, in the soup, over the potatoes.

Potassium deserves a mention, since it does the other half of the job. It's in the plain stuff: potatoes, beans, yogurt, bananas, leafy greens, avocado. If your meals have some of those most days, you've handled it without a single scoop of powder — which is another argument for eating on something like a schedule, made in Eating on a Rhythm, Not a Clock. The coffee half of the story lives in Caffeine Timing Without the Dogma.

The honest summary is unexciting. Drink when you're thirsty. Eat food that has minerals in it. Pay a little more attention on the hot, sweaty days and a lot less on the ordinary ones. Steady days rarely come from a rule you're enforcing. They come from a setup where the right thing happens anyway, and you stop having to think about it.

Disclaimer: This is educational content, not medical advice. Fluid and sodium needs vary from person to person, and anyone managing blood pressure, kidney or heart conditions, or taking medications that affect fluid balance should talk with a qualified healthcare professional before making deliberate changes to water or salt intake.
