Evidence

Supplements and the Evidence Gap

By the Simply Wellness team May 18, 2026 9 min read

A bottle on a shelf carries a quiet implication: that somebody checked. The truth sits between “unregulated” and “tested like medicine.”

A bottle on a shelf carries a quiet implication: that somebody, somewhere, checked. The pill exists, it has a name, it lists a dose, it sits three feet from the pharmacy counter — surely a gate was passed. That assumption is the most common misunderstanding in the category, and it is worth unpicking, because the truth is neither "supplements are unregulated" nor "supplements are tested like medicines." It is a third thing, and the third thing is where the confusion lives.

The category was invented, deliberately

In the United States, dietary supplements occupy a legal space carved out by a 1994 statute that placed them closer to food than to medicine. The practical consequence is a reversal of order. A new prescription drug must demonstrate to a regulator, before sale, that it does something and that its risks are acceptable. A supplement generally does not. Responsibility for safety sits with the manufacturer, and the agency's main lever arrives afterward — action against a product already on the market, usually once a problem has surfaced. Britain and the European Union run different machinery with a comparable gap: food supplements sit under food law, with a list of permitted wordings, rather than the approval pipeline used for medicines.

This is not a scandal and not a secret. It is a policy choice about a category holding both a multivitamin and a concentrated botanical nobody had heard of five years ago. But it means the shelf itself certifies very little.

Read the claim, not the packaging

The wording on a label is more carefully engineered than most shoppers realize. There is a legal distinction between saying a product "supports normal immune function" and saying it "treats infections." The first is a structure or function claim; the second is a disease claim, and a disease claim pulls a product into drug territory. The industry has become fluent in the first kind, and the asterisk near the bottom of the panel — noting the statement has not been evaluated by the regulator — is the tell.

Once a reader sees that phrasing is a compliance instrument rather than a summary of findings, the aisle reads differently. "Supports." "Helps maintain." "Promotes." These are not stronger claims trimmed for modesty. They are the strongest claims permitted without evidence of the kind medicines must produce.

The gap is not between supplements and drugs. It is between what a bottle is required to prove and what the person holding it assumes has already been proven.

What "studies show" is usually showing

Almost every product has a research page. The question is what kind of research got listed there, and the honest answer, most of the time, is: the early kind.

  • Cell studies. An isolated molecule shifts a marker on a lab bench, usually at a concentration no capsule could ever deliver to a living person.
  • Animal studies. Useful for direction, weak for translation. The dose is frequently scaled in ways that would be implausible in a person.
  • Small, short human trials. Thirty participants over four weeks, measuring a blood marker rather than anything a person would notice.
  • Reviews of the above. A review of thin studies is still thin. Pooling does not manufacture rigor.
  • Trials funded by the seller. Not automatically wrong, but a reason to read the methods section rather than the press release.

None of these is illegitimate science. They are simply early links in a chain that mostly does not get finished, because finishing it is expensive and — for an ingredient nobody can patent — commercially pointless. The result is a literature that is genuinely large and genuinely inconclusive at the same time. That mismatch is the same trap described in why single studies rarely settle anything.

The dose in the paper and the dose in the bottle

Even when a decent human trial exists, it may not be a trial of the product being sold. This is the least intuitive part of the whole subject.

Turmeric is the standard example. Most of the research uses curcumin extracts formulated for absorption — bound to a carrier, paired with a pepper compound, or delivered in a particle small enough to survive the gut. Plain powdered turmeric behaves quite differently in the body. A label can cite a study honestly and still contain something the study did not test. The same problem recurs across botanicals, where the plant part, the extraction solvent, and the standardized concentration all matter and none is guaranteed to match.

Worth knowing: third-party certification marks — the kind from independent testing organizations — verify identity, purity, and that the contents match the label. That is a real service, and it is worth paying attention to. It is not a statement about whether the ingredient does anything. Clean and useful are separate questions, and only one of them gets a seal.

Where the ground is firmer

Skepticism about the category is not the same as skepticism about every product in it. The clearest case for supplementation is correcting a documented shortfall, and that case is fairly well established: folic acid before and during early pregnancy, vitamin B12 for people who eat no animal foods, vitamin D in places where winter sun is genuinely scarce, iron when a blood test and a clinician say so.

Notice what those have in common. Each targets a specific deficiency, in a specific population, at a dose someone can justify. That is a narrower proposition than "supports vitality," and it is exactly why it holds up. The framing failure is the same one that afflicts the word "superfood": a real, bounded finding gets stretched into a general promise, and the stretching is where the evidence quietly disappears.

The common claimWhat actually holds up
"If it's on the shelf, someone verified it works."Efficacy review generally happens before sale for medicines, not for supplements. Presence on a shelf is not a finding.
"Studies show it works."Often true and nearly uninformative. The relevant questions are: which species, how many people, how long, funded by whom, measuring what.
"It's natural, so the risk is low."Dose and interaction determine risk, not origin. Concentrated botanicals can interfere with prescription medicines.
"The label matches the research."Frequently the trial used a different formulation, extract, or dose than the one in the bottle.
"A certification seal means it's effective."Seals attest to contents and purity. They are silent on whether the contents help.

Five questions before the checkout

  1. What specific shortfall is this meant to address, and is there any reason to think it exists here?
  2. Does the cited research involve people, at this dose, in this form — or cells, mice, and an extract with another name?
  3. Who paid for the study, and was it registered before it started?
  4. What does it interact with? This is the question for a pharmacist, and pharmacists answer it for free.
  5. What would count as it not working — and is that even measurable from the outside?

The last one tends to be the most revealing. A product whose benefit cannot fail to appear is not offering evidence. It is offering an unfalsifiable feeling, which is a fine thing to buy as long as everyone is clear that is the purchase. Origin claims deserve the same scrutiny, since natural does not mean gentle either.
